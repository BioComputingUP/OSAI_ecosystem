Challenges in Life Science AI Research

Artificial intelligence is revolutionsing the life sciences, but its rapid adoption has created significant challenges. Many new AI models are difficult to reuse, their results are hard to reproduce, and their environmental impact is often overlooked. This hinders scientific progress and leads to wasted resources.

To address this, we've identified three key areas that need our collective focus:

Reusability

Many AI models and datasets are difficult to discover, access, and adapt for new research, leading to duplicate efforts and slower progress.

Reproducibility

Incomplete documentation and missing code dependencies make it hard to verify results and build upon previous work.

Sustainability

The growing environmental footprint of AI training and deployment is rarely measured or optimised, contributing to unnecessary carbon emissions.

Guidelines for Open and Sustainable AI (OSAI)

We propose nine practical recommendations to guide researchers in creating AI models that are more open and sustainable. These recommendations are grouped into three core themes and are designed to be actionable, supported by a wider ecosystem of tools and resources.

They follow a hierarchy of prioritisation with guiding tags as follows:

ESSENTIAL

Core practices - fundamental for achieving base Open and Sustainable AI.

REQUIRED

Important practices - needed for robust Open and Sustainable AI outputs.

RECOMMENDED

Additional practices - for fully achieving Open and Sustainable AI.

Reusability

To ensure that AI models and datasets can be effectively shared, understood, and adapted for new research.

R1: Generate and share standardised AI metadata for models and datasets

Adopt community-driven standards like FAIR4ML for models and Croissant for datasets to improve how they can be found and used by others. This makes your work more discoverable and interoperable.

Why it matters: Standardised metadata makes it easier for others to find, understand, and properly use your AI models and datasets, increasing their impact and reusability.
ESSENTIAL

R2: Leverage AI registries as central hubs for sharing and discovering reusable AI models and datasets

Deposit your models and datasets in established registries (e.g., BioImage Model Zoo, Hugging Face) to increase their visibility and accessibility within the scientific community.

Why it matters: Registries provide a structured way to share your work, making it more discoverable and ensuring it remains available long-term for others to build upon.
REQUIRED

R3: Host, promote and share training and guidance on correct deposition of AI research objects for reuse

Develop and share training materials on best practices for depositing AI models and datasets. Use open platforms to make this knowledge accessible to everyone.

Why it matters: Training materials help build community capacity and ensure that researchers understand how to properly share their work for maximum reuse.
RECOMMENDED

Reproducibility

To ensure that research findings can be independently verified and built upon by other researchers.

R4: Ensure transparent disclosure, clear documentation and sharing of all model relevant information

Provide comprehensive details about your model architecture, training data, and evaluation methods. Use checklists like the Model Openness Framework and DOME to guide your documentation.

Why it matters: Without thorough documentation, others cannot understand how your model works or why it produces certain results, making it difficult to verify or build upon.
ESSENTIAL

R5: Provide portable code and reproducible environments to facilitate smooth reuse

Use tools like Docker, Conda, and Nextflow to package your code and its dependencies. This allows other researchers to more easily run and reproduce your work across different systems.

Why it matters: Code that runs on your system but fails on others due to environment differences is a major barrier to reproduction. Containerisation and workflows solve this problem.
REQUIRED

R6: Use standardised AI-ready datasets and benchmarking evaluation protocols to facilitate reproducible model comparisons

Use community-accepted benchmark datasets and evaluation protocols to ensure that your model's performance can be fairly and accurately compared to others.

Why it matters: Standardised benchmarking enables fair comparisons between models and prevents selective reporting that may exaggerate performance claims.
RECOMMENDED

Environmental Sustainability

To minimse the environmental footprint of AI research by promoting energy-efficient practices.

R7: Implement Green AI model development techniques

Reduce energy consumption by using techniques like transfer learning, pruning, and knowledge distillation. Adopt "Frugal AI" principles to achieve better results with less computational power.

Why it matters: Computationally efficient models not only reduce environmental impact but can also be more accessible to researchers with limited computing resources.
ESSENTIAL

R8: Choose and optimise hardware to reduce environmental impact

Select energy-efficient hardware and, when using cloud services, consider data centers powered by green energy.

Why it matters: The hardware used for training and inference can significantly affect energy consumption. Making informed choices can substantially reduce carbon emissions.
REQUIRED
