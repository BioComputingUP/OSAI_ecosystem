Reusability Pathway

After investing time creating an AI model and corresponding dataset, it is important to ensure these are reusable in order to maximise the impact of your work. This crucial step is vital to ensure that other researchers in the scientific community can easily find, access, and understand your outputs for reuse. This example pathway outlines the key steps leveraging OSAI reuse guidelines (R1-R3) to effectively share your AI model and dataset for reuse, thereby enhancing collaboration and accelerating scientific progress.

1 Learn Reuse Best Practices Through Training (R3)

Before sharing your model and data, it's beneficial to understand the established best practices for making research outputs reusable. Several resources provide guidance on data and software management which will set the foundation for metadata generation in Step 2 of this pathway.

Example Resources:
RDMkit (Research Data Management Toolkit) Example Guidance
This toolkit offers comprehensive guidance on managing research data throughout its lifecycle. While not exclusively focused on AI, its principles are fundamental for preparing your AI datasets for sharing.
RSQkit (Research Software Quality Toolkit) Example Guidance
As AI models are a form of research software, this toolkit provides valuable best practices for ensuring your model's code is high-quality, well-documented, and sustainable for long-term reuse.
SciLifeLab Funder requirements and FAIR ML models Example Guidance
This is a practical example of guidance from a national life science organisation on how to make machine learning models Open and FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable). It can serve as a template for meeting the requirements of funders and institutions.

2 Create Comprehensive Metadata Based on Standards (R1)

After Step 1 of this pathway, you will have learned about different types of metadata. In this context, the metadata generated acts as the data describing your AI model and dataset. It is essential for AI asset reusability as it provides the necessary context for others to discover, comprehend, and support trust in your work. It allows both humans and machines to interact with them in a structured and consistent way when using metadata standards.

Before you start, it's wise to identify a few potential registries where you plan to deposit your model and dataset (see Step 3). Review their specific metadata requirements to ensure you capture all necessary information from the outset, which will save you time and effort later.

Example Resources:
Metadata for Your Model:
Model Cards Example Implementation
This is a documentation framework for reporting on a model's intended uses, performance, limitations, and ethical considerations. It provides a standardised summary for potential users.
ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange) Example Implementation
While a format for model interoperability, ONNX files also embed important metadata about the model's architecture, inputs, and outputs, which is crucial for practical reuse.
Metadata for Your Dataset:
Croissant Example Implementation
A metadata format from MLCommons designed to make ML datasets easily discoverable, understandable, and usable across different tools and platforms.
Dataset Cards Example Implementation
Similar to Model Cards, these provide a structured way to document a dataset's origins, contents, collection methods, and potential biases, helping others assess its suitability for their needs.

3 Deposit in an Open and Accessible AI Registry (R2)

Once your AI model and related dataset are well-documented with rich metadata (Step 2), the final step is to place them in a public AI registry to make them findable and accessible to the wider community. These repositories are crucial for disseminating AI models and datasets. Choose a registry according to aspects such as your target user audience, domain specificity of the registry, and optimal functionality (e.g. based on support for your metadata).

Example Resources:
Registries for Your Model:
Hugging Face Example Implementation
A widely used platform that serves as a central hub for sharing and discovering open-source models, especially in the domain of Natural Language Processing. It has strong community features and integrates well with many popular ML libraries.
Ersilia Model Hub Example Implementation
A specialised registry focusing on pre-trained AI/ML models for drug discovery and infectious disease research, making it an excellent choice for models within this life science domain.
Zenodo Example Implementation
A general-purpose open-access repository, operated by CERN, that can be used to share any research output, including AI models. It is particularly useful for obtaining a persistent Digital Object Identifier (DOI) for your model, which makes it easily citable.
Registries for Your Dataset:
BioImage Model Zoo Example Implementation
A community-driven repository primarily for sharing pre-trained artificial intelligence models for bioimage analysis. It can host reference datasets used to train and test these models, linking models to their corresponding data.
Figshare Example Implementation
An online digital repository where researchers can preserve and share their research outputs, including datasets, figures, and papers, with persistent identifiers.
Kaggle Example Implementation
A platform that hosts a vast collection of datasets, often used for machine learning competitions and data science practice. It fosters a large community for collaboration and provides integrated cloud-based tools for data analysis.

Reproducibility Pathway

To ensure the findings from your AI model are credible and can be built upon by the scientific community, it is essential to move beyond simple reusability and strive for full reproducibility. This pathway guides you through the process of standardising your evaluations, packaging your code for easy execution, and transparently disclosing all relevant details in alignment with OSAI recommendations (R4-6) so that others can independently verify and replicate your results.

1 Standardise Datasets for Reproducible Comparisons (R6)

Before considering wider reproducibility of your AI model by others (Steps 2 & 3), it is crucial to have reproducible model evaluation results for others to cross-compare to and ensure your model performance claims are valid. Instead of using personally prepared datasets for final evaluations of your AI model, you should benchmark it against established AI-ready datasets. These are datasets that have been prepared for such purposes and are widely used by the community, reducing variability in evaluation.

Platforms hosting community benchmarking challenges are excellent sources for standardised datasets and evaluation protocols. Participating in or using resources from these challenges ensures your model is assessed against the current state-of-the-art in a controlled and transparent manner.

Benchmarking services like OpenEBench serve as central hubs in the life sciences for discovering benchmarking challenges and accessing their standardised evaluation results and datasets. Using such platforms helps you to align your work with community best practices to ensure accurate and reproducible evaluation reporting.

Example Resources:
Kaggle Competitions Example Implementation
A domain-agnostic platform hosting a vast number of competitions with standardised datasets and evaluation metrics that can be used for benchmarking.
OpenEBench Example Implementation
A platform for scientific benchmarking that helps researchers to validate and select the best tools for their analyses in the life sciences.
CAID (Critical Assessment of Intrinsic Disorder prediction) Example Implementation
A life-science-specific challenge that provides datasets and standardised protocols for models that predict intrinsically disordered regions in proteins.


Note: Where there are no available AI-ready benchmarking datasets for your model, make this clear and openly share your dataset in relevant AI registries for its reuse. Guidance on this is provided under the Reusability Pathway (Steps 2 & 3).

2 Create Reproducible Environments and Workflows (R5)

Once your model has been fairly benchmarked for reproducible results (Step 1), the next step is to package the entire computational environment and use a workflow management system so that others can execute your code with minimal effort and achieve the same results. It is advised to implement the following technologies at the start of your AI model creation, as it is harder to implement them after the fact.

2.1. Ensure a compatible software environment: This foundational step involves meticulously defining and isolating all software dependencies, such as libraries, tools, and their specific versions. Properly managing these components prevents conflicts and ensures your code runs consistently across different machines, forming the bedrock of a reproducible workflow.

Before containerising, use a dedicated environment control manager to create self-contained environments for your project's software packages. This is the first line of defence against dependency conflicts and simplifies sharing the exact software requirements with others.

Example Resources:
Conda Example Implementation
An open-source package and environment management system popular in data science. It excels at installing packages and managing their complex dependencies across Windows, macOS, and Linux.
Pixi Example Implementation
A modern, fast package manager and workflow tool built on Conda's ecosystem. It uses a single pixi.toml file to manage cross-platform dependencies and tasks, simplifying project setup and collaboration.

2.2. Containerise the Environment: The key step in achieving reproducibility is containerisation, which bundles your code with all its software dependencies (libraries, tools, and specific versions) into a single, portable image. This eliminates the common problem of "but it works on my machine" by ensuring an identical environment across systems.

Example Resources:
Docker Example Implementation
The most widely used containerisation platform, ideal for creating and sharing application environments.
Apptainer (formerly Singularity) Example Implementation
A container system popular in academic and High-Performance Computing (HPC) environments, designed with a focus on security for multi-user systems.

2.3. Orchestrate All Model Steps with a Workflow Management System:
For complex AI models that involve multiple steps (e.g., preprocessing, training, inference, and evaluation), a workflow management system is crucial. These systems take your containerised components and automate the execution of the entire pipeline in the correct order, ensuring a complete and seamless reproduction of your AI model.

Example Resources:
Nextflow Example Implementation
A powerful and scalable workflow system that excels at managing complex bioinformatics pipelines and integrates seamlessly with container technologies.
Galaxy Example Implementation
A web-based platform that allows researchers to build, run, and share complex computational analyses, including machine learning workflows, in a highly accessible and reproducible manner.

3 Transparently Disclose and Document AI Methods (R4)

Finally, with a standardised evaluation (Step 1) and technical support for the reproducibility of your model in place (Step 2), the final step is to create comprehensive documentation that clearly discloses all information necessary in a structured manner for someone to understand and reproduce your work.

3.1. Consult & use AI disclosure frameworks:
Many established AI method disclosure checklists and recommendations exist to guide researchers on what information is essential to disclose in order to aid reproducibility. These frameworks provide a structured guide to ensure you don't miss sharing any critical details. Choice may be influenced by your specific research domain, target audience, or journal requirements (e.g. GigaScience journal requires use of the DOME standard).

Example Resources:
DOME Recommendations Example Implementation
Community-driven guidelines for reporting supervised machine learning in biology, covering aspects from data processing to model evaluation.
Model Openness Framework (MOF) Example Implementation
A domain-agnostic checklist for evaluating the openness and completeness of a machine learning model, covering code, data, and documentation.
Bridge2AI Recommendations Example Implementation
NIH guidance including best practices for making biomedical data AI-ready and for transparently documenting AI methodologies.
3.2. Disclose Information Alongside Your Research Assets:
  • In the methods section of your publication.
  • As supplementary material linked to your publication.
  • Within the README.md file of your public code repository.
Example Resources:
GitHub Example Implementation
The most widely used platform for sharing code and documentation, ideal for hosting your README.md and supplementary files for reproducibility.
GitLab Example Implementation
An open-source alternative to GitHub, supporting public and private repositories for sharing code, documentation, and reproducibility assets.
3.3. Leverage Dedicated AI Method Structured Disclosure Services:
 For an even higher level of transparency and findability, use specialised registries designed to capture structured information about your study.
Example Resource:
QsarDB Example Implementation
A public repository for Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship (QSAR) models and data. It provides a structured format for storing and sharing QSAR studies, ensuring that the data, models, and validation results are transparent and reproducible.
DOME Registry Example Implementation
Allows you to create a structured, human and machine-readable transparent documentation of your machine learning study. It complements your publication by providing curated, persistent annotations based on the DOME recommendations, directly linking all components of your work and maximising its visibility and reproducibility potential.

Environmental Sustainability Pathway

Achieving results with AI should not come at an unnecessary cost to our planet. By embedding environmental sustainability into your research practice, you can minimise the carbon footprint of your work while promoting a more responsible and efficient approach to AI development in alignment with OSAI recommendations (R6-9). This pathway will guide you through implementing Green AI techniques, making informed hardware choices, and transparently reporting your model's environmental impact.

1 Implement Green AI Software Development Techniques (R7)

The most direct way to reduce your environmental impact is to decrease the amount of computation required to train and run your AI model. Adopting "Green AI" software development practices focuses on efficiency, ensuring you achieve your research goals with the least amount of energy possible before having to consider hardware optimisation (Step 2).

1.1. Select Efficient Algorithms and Models: Before starting, consider the inherent efficiency of the algorithms you plan to use. Some models are less computationally intensive by design. For tabular data, libraries like XGBoost and LightGBM are not only high-performing but are also optimised for speed and efficiency, making them a greener choice than more complex deep learning models where not necessary.

Example Resource:
XGBoost Example Implementation
A high-performance, efficient library for gradient boosting on tabular data, optimised for speed and resource usage.

1.2. Apply Model Optimisation Techniques: You can significantly reduce the computational cost of deep learning models by making them smaller and more efficient without a major loss in performance. Techniques like pruning (removing unnecessary model parameters) and quantisation (using lower-precision numerical formats) can drastically cut down on resource needs. The EfficientBioAI package is an example of a tool designed specifically to implement such methods for bio-imaging AI tasks.

Example Resource:
EfficientBioAI Example Implementation
A package for bio-imaging AI that implements pruning and quantisation techniques to reduce model size and energy consumption.

1.3. Adopt Energy-Efficient Development Practices: How you develop your model matters just as much as the model itself. Consult established guides to learn about and apply these practices throughout your project lifecycle. The Green Software Patterns guide, from the Green Software Foundation, provides a collection of actionable strategies, such as efficient data loading, energy-aware hyperparameter tuning, and leveraging transfer learning from pre-trained models to avoid training from scratch.

Example Resource:
Green Software Patterns Example Implementation
A guide from the Green Software Foundation with practical strategies for energy-efficient AI development, including efficient data loading and transfer learning.

Specific Green AI Model Development Techniques:

Transfer Learning Example Implementation
Using a pre-trained model as a starting point for a new task, leveraging its learned features to save the massive energy cost of training from scratch.
Fine-Tuning Example Implementation
Further training a pre-trained model on a new, smaller dataset to adapt its specialised knowledge to a specific task, which is far more efficient than full training.
Efficient & Specialised Architectures Example Implementation
Intentionally designing or choosing smaller, simpler models that are focused on a narrow task to reduce computational demand.
Model Optimisation (Quantisation & Pruning) Example Implementation
Making models smaller and faster by reducing the numerical precision (quantisation) and removing non-essential parts (pruning).
Knowledge Distillation Example Implementation
Training a compact "student" model to mimic a larger "teacher" model, transferring its capabilities into a more efficient form.
Efficient Hyperparameter Optimisation Example Implementation
Using intelligent search methods to find the best model settings in fewer attempts, saving significant computational effort.

2 Choose and Optimise Hardware for AI (R8)

After taking appropriate action to identify the relevant Green AI software development techniques you will use (Step 1), the next major concern is the choice of hardware you use. This will relate to factors such as how it is optimised, where it is located and time of day you run your workloads which are all critical factors that influence your project's energy consumption and related carbon footprint.

On-Premises and Local Computing: If you are using your institution's hardware, there are still choices you can make to optimise for sustainability. Use modern, energy-efficient hardware like GPUs for deep learning, optimise the hardware settings of the available infrastructure, and consider the energy efficiency of the supercomputing centres you use by consulting resources like the Top 500 Green list.

Example Resources:
NVIDIA GPU Cloud (NGC)
A hub for GPU-optimised software that accelerates AI on energy-efficient GPUs.
POP CoE
Provides free services to help academic and industrial users optimise their HPC applications for better performance and efficiency.

Cloud Computing: Cloud platforms offer significant flexibility for reducing your carbon footprint. You can choose to run your computations in data centres powered by a higher percentage of renewable energy and schedule your jobs to run at times when the local electricity grid is less carbon-intensive.

Example Resources:
Carbon free energy for Google Cloud
Guidance from Google to help users select cloud regions with a higher percentage of carbon-free energy.
Carbon Aware SDK
A toolkit that allows applications to be scheduled and run during times of lower grid carbon intensity.

3 Measure and Report AI Model Environmental Impact (R9)

Finally, to understand your project's environmental footprint and contribute to a community-wide culture of transparency after reducing environmental impacts (Steps 1 & 2), it is essential to measure and report your energy consumption and carbon emissions. This should be planned from the very beginning of your project.

3.1.1. Use Active Monitoring Tools (Preferred): Integrate monitoring tools directly into your code to automatically track energy consumption and estimate the carbon footprint of your training and inference runs in real-time. Cloud platforms also offer built-in dashboards to monitor your carbon footprint.

Example Resources:
CodeCarbon Example Implementation
An open-source Python package to track and visualise the energy consumption and CO2 emissions of your code.
Experiment Impact Tracker Example Implementation
A tool to track energy usage, carbon emissions, and compute utilisation of your experiments.
Carbon Tracker Example Implementation
A tool designed to predict and track the carbon footprint of training deep learning models.

3.1.2. Estimate Impact When Active Monitoring Is Not Possible: If you cannot use active monitoring tools, you can still create an estimate after your experiments are complete. This requires you to diligently log key details during your runs: the specific hardware used, the total runtime, and the geographic location of the hardware. This information can then be input into an online calculator.

Example Resource:
ML CO2 Impact Calculator Example Implementation
A calculator for estimating the carbon footprint of machine learning experiments based on hardware, runtime, and location.

3.2. Report Your Findings: Finally, make your environmental impact metrics public. Disclose them in the methods section of your publication or in your code repository. For a greater community impact, submit your results to dedicated platforms that track and compare the energy efficiency of models, such as the Reinforcement Learning Energy Leaderboard or the Machine Learning Energy Leaderboard.

Example Resources:
Reinforcement Learning Energy Leaderboard Example Implementation
A public leaderboard that tracks and compares the energy efficiency and carbon footprint of reinforcement learning models and algorithms.
Machine Learning Energy Leaderboard Example Implementation
A platform for reporting and comparing the energy consumption of machine learning models, helping researchers promote transparency and sustainability.

OSAI Implementation Example: APNet

Read the Paper

APNet is an explainable sparse deep learning model designed to discover differentially active drivers of severe COVID-19. To help contextualise a basic implementation of the OSAI Recommendations (R1-9), the APNet model is used as a toy example of a minimal OSAI-aligned AI method to guide first time adopters.

Note: Many OSAI areas are directly covered by this APNet example. However, for certain OSAI Recommendations where they were not fully met by the original publication, they are provided with additional example context to ensure full OSAI coverage and aid prospective implementers.

Reusability

R1 - Standardised Metadata: A standardised model metadata file was generated to ensure the methods were human and machine-readable. The FAIR4ML standard was used for the APNet model and FAIR4ML JSON file made available in the GitHub. The Croissant standard was used for the AI dataset metadata covering the Olink proteomic datasets (MGH, Mayo, Stanford) and scRNAseq datasets (scMGH, Blish). These were all made available in the GitHub to support reuse at source.

R2 - Use of Registries: To guarantee long-term access, the stable version of the research objects was registered in Zenodo (generating a persistent DOI), while the active development codebase is maintained on GitHub.

R3 - Reuse Training: Comprehensive documentation and a tutorial were hosted directly on the GitHub repository. This guides users on how to properly reuse the datasets, model and how to contribute back to the project to extend the methods.

Reproducibility

R4 - Transparent Methods: The DOME checklist was followed to transparently document all key aspects of the study. The full disclosure was made available on the DOME Registry and covers aspects such as disclosure on the PASNet architecture, biological priors used (WikiPathways, KEGG, Reactome, GO), and specific evaluation methods across datasets (Evaluation 1: Validation on Mayo/Testing on Stanford; Evaluation 2: Validation on scMGH/Testing on Blish).

R5 - Portable Environments: The computational environment was made to be portable across systems by providing a Conda configuration file (environment.yml), Docker container files, and step-by-step build and installation instructions provided in the README in the GitHub.

R6 - Standardised Evaluation: Ablation studies and relevant benchmark evaluations were performed using expression matrices to ensure performance comparisons against other models were fair and reproducible. This was shared and made available in the publication and on the GitHub.

Environmental Sustainability

R7 - Green AI Techniques: Model complexity and computational cost were actively reduced by adopting a Green AI technique of a sparse neural network architecture informed by pathway-based biological priors, rather than using a fully connected dense network.

R8 - Hardware Choice: The efficient hardware choice strategy was optimised by running lightweight experiments on Google Colab's AI efficient TPUs and reserving energy-intensive tasks, such as hyperparameter grid searches, for AI optimised High-Performance Computing (HPC) infrastructure.

R9 - Measure & Report: A lightweight carbon and energy impact measuring tool (Green Algorithms calculator) was implemented to estimate and report on estimated energy use (kWh) and carbon emissions (COe) for both the Google Colab & final HPC model training runs. This information was captured as 45.94 gCO2e and 108.36 Wh for the Colab-based analyses and 2.570 kgCO2e and 6.280 kWh for the main HPC training. This information was made available in the publication and on the GitHub.
